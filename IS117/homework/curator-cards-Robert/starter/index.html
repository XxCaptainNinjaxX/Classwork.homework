Design Movements: Curator Cards

Bauhaus (1919–1933)

Founded by Walter Gropius in Weimar, Germany, the Bauhaus school radically unified fine art, craft, and mass production. Operating on the principle that form follows function, the institution stripped away historical ornamentation in favor of functional, geometric simplicity. Through its foundational Vorkurs curriculum, students mastered primary colors, unadorned materials, and spatial relationships. The school revolutionized typography by championing lowercase, sans-serif typefaces that prioritized universal communication over decorative tradition. Although forced to close by the Nazi regime in 1933, the emigration of its masters embedded modernist design principles deeply into global architecture and graphic design education.

Tags: Vorkurs · form follows function · geometric abstraction · universal communication · sans-serif typography

Examples: MoMA Bauhaus Collection | Harvard Busch-Reisinger

Not to be confused with: Art Deco, because while both existed in the 1920s, Art Deco focused on luxury and ornamentation, whereas Bauhaus focused on stark minimalism.

Accountability: I verified the founding date and core philosophy by checking the MoMA educational archives on Walter Gropius.

Swiss Style (1950s–1970s)

Emerging from Switzerland after WWII, the International Typographic Style prioritized absolute clarity, objectivity, and rational order in visual communication. Designers rejected subjective illustration, favoring instead objective photography and highly structured mathematical grids. This movement established asymmetric layouts as a standard for dynamic tension while maintaining total legibility. The philosophy relied heavily on sans-serif typefaces—most notably Akzidenz-Grotesk and later Helvetica—to present information neutrally without the designer's personal expression getting in the way. It became the definitive corporate design language of the mid-20th century.

Tags: mathematical grid · objective photography · asymmetric layout · Akzidenz-Grotesk · rational order

Examples: Design Museum London | Museum für Gestaltung

Not to be confused with: De Stijl, because while both use strict geometry, Swiss Style prioritizes objective information delivery, whereas De Stijl is a purely abstract art movement.

Accountability: I verified the use of mathematical grids and Akzidenz-Grotesk via the Museum für Gestaltung Zürich's poster collection history.

Reaction to Swiss: Grunge Typography (1990s)

Exploding out of the 1990s alternative music and skateboarding scenes, Grunge typography was a direct rebellion against the sterile, hyper-rational constraints of the Swiss Style. Pioneered by designers like David Carson in publications such as Ray Gun, this movement treated type as an expressive, texture-heavy image rather than just a vehicle for reading. By embracing anti-legibility, designers purposefully overlapped text, ignored grid systems entirely, and utilized distressed textures and erratic baselines. The philosophy argued that emotional resonance and visual intuition were vastly more important than corporate clarity.

Tags: anti-legibility · expressive typography · distressed textures · erratic baselines · Ray Gun

Examples: Cooper Hewitt: David Carson | AIGA: David Carson Medalist

Not to be confused with: New Wave Typography, because while both broke the Swiss grid, New Wave remained rooted in academic theory, whereas Grunge actively sought chaotic, intuitive destruction.

Accountability: I verified David Carson's involvement with Ray Gun magazine via his AIGA Medalist biography.
